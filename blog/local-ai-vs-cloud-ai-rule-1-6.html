Local AI vs. Cloud AI: A Rule 1.6 Decision Framework for Attorneys

May 11, 2026  ·  7 min read
← Back to Blog

You're considering an AI tool for your practice. Maybe you've seen colleagues using one, or you've tried a consumer product and recognized its potential. At some point you hit a question that stops most attorneys cold: What happens to my client's data?

That question has a real answer — but the answer differs dramatically depending on whether you're looking at a cloud-based AI or a locally-run one. Understanding the distinction isn't just a technical matter. It's an ethics issue, and Rule 1.6 is the controlling authority.

What Rule 1.6 Actually Requires

ABA Model Rule 1.6(c) requires that attorneys "make reasonable efforts to prevent the inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure of, or unauthorized access to, information relating to the representation." The standard is reasonable efforts — not perfection, not absolute security, but a deliberate analysis of the risks.

ABA Formal Opinion 512 (July 2024) extended this obligation explicitly to AI tools. Before submitting client information to any AI system, you must evaluate the likelihood of disclosure, the sensitivity of the data, the difficulty of safeguards, and whether those safeguards would impair your representation. That analysis plays out very differently for cloud-based versus local AI.

How Cloud AI Works — and Where the Risk Lives

Cloud AI tools — including most of the well-known large language models — process your prompts on remote servers operated by third-party providers. When you type a prompt containing client facts, case details, or document content, that text leaves your machine, travels to the provider's infrastructure, and is processed there.

The Rule 1.6 risks concentrate at three points:

Data retention. Many providers retain conversation history and user inputs for periods ranging from days to indefinitely. Even providers with "no training" commitments may still retain data for abuse monitoring, debugging, or other operational purposes. The key question is not whether they claim not to train on your data — it's what their terms of service and data processing addendum actually commit to, in writing.

Model training. Consumer-tier plans for most major AI products default to using your conversations to improve their models. Legal-specific or enterprise tiers typically offer opt-out provisions or explicit commitments against training on your data. If you're using a consumer plan, check this before your next prompt involving a client matter.

Third-party access. Cloud infrastructure involves multiple parties: the AI provider, their cloud hosting vendor, any subprocessors they engage. A provider's own data handling practices may be excellent while still exposing data to infrastructure partners whose practices you haven't vetted. Opinion 512 treats this chain of custody as part of your confidentiality analysis.

For cloud AI to be Rule 1.6-compliant, you need: a legal-tier or enterprise subscription, a data processing agreement, explicit no-training commitments, and a clear picture of what the provider's subprocessors can access. That's achievable — but only if you've deliberately set it up that way.

How Local AI Works — and What It Changes

Local AI runs entirely on your own hardware. The model weights are downloaded to your machine; inference happens on your CPU or GPU; nothing leaves your network. There is no third-party server receiving your prompt. There is no provider to have a data processing agreement with, because there is no provider in the loop.

From a Rule 1.6 standpoint, local AI eliminates the third-party disclosure risk category entirely. Client information stays on your machine, subject to whatever security controls you've applied to your own infrastructure.

The practical tradeoffs are real, though:

  • Hardware requirements. Running capable local AI models requires a reasonably modern machine with sufficient RAM (16–32 GB is a common floor for useful models). More capable models need more resources.
  • Model capability. Local models currently lag behind frontier cloud models on complex reasoning tasks. The gap is narrowing, but it exists. For straightforward drafting tasks, the gap may be irrelevant; for complex analysis, you may notice it.
  • Setup complexity. Tools like Ollama have made local AI more accessible than it was two years ago, but it still requires more technical comfort than signing up for a web app.

The Decision Framework

Not every client matter carries the same confidentiality weight. The Rule 1.6 analysis should be proportionate to the sensitivity of the information at stake.

Task Sensitivity Examples Recommended Approach
Lower General legal research, drafting from public sources, template generation with no client facts Any reputable cloud tool with reasonable terms
Moderate Drafting correspondence with general case posture, reviewing standard forms without identifying details Cloud with verified DPA + no-training commitment, or capable local model
High Client-identifying documents, financial records, medical details, detailed case strategy, privileged communications Enterprise cloud with ZDR, or local AI
Highly sensitive Domestic violence, immigration status, sealed proceedings, corporate insider information Local AI or strict zero-retention cloud only

The framework isn't binary. You don't have to choose between cloud and local for your entire practice. You can use each where the risk calculus supports it — and many attorneys do exactly that.

What This Means Practically

A few concrete steps that follow from this analysis:

Audit what you're currently using. If you're already using an AI tool, pull up its terms of service and find the section on data handling, retention, and training. If that section doesn't clearly say your data won't be used to train models, assume it will be.

Get the right tier. Most reputable AI platforms offer professional or legal tiers specifically designed to provide the data handling commitments attorneys need. The upgrade cost is typically modest relative to what's at stake.

Document your analysis. The ethics rules require reasonable efforts, which implies a conscious decision. Keep a brief memo to file explaining what AI tools you use for what kinds of tasks and why you've concluded the data handling is adequate. If the question ever comes up, you want to be able to show you thought about it.

Consider local for your most sensitive work. Even if you use cloud AI for most tasks, having a local model available for high-sensitivity matters is increasingly practical and worth the setup investment.

The choice between local and cloud AI is less a technical decision than an ethical workflow decision. Rule 1.6 doesn't prohibit cloud AI — it requires that you do the analysis. Do it deliberately, document it, and revisit it as the tools and the guidance evolve.
