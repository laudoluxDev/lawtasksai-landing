Why This Matters to You

OpenClaw is a powerful AI assistant that can help with legal research, document drafting, calendar management, and more. But because it processes text you give it — potentially including client names, case details, and privileged communications — it must be treated with the same care as any system that handles confidential client information.

This guide explains, in plain language, what you need to do to keep your clients’ information safe, whether OpenClaw runs on your office computer or on a virtual machine in Google’s cloud. You don’t need to understand the technical details yourself — your developer handles implementation — but you do need to understand what protections are in place and why they matter.

Important This guide addresses security configuration — protecting the system that runs OpenClaw. It does not address whether specific uses of AI comply with all applicable ethical rules. You should also review your obligations regarding competence, client communication, supervision, and billing before using any AI tool in your practice.

Your Ethical Obligations Under ABA Formal Opinion 512

In July 2024, the ABA Standing Committee on Ethics and Professional Responsibility published Formal Opinion 512, its first formal guidance on lawyers’ use of generative AI. The opinion maps existing Model Rules of Professional Conduct onto the new reality of AI-assisted legal work.

The Model Rules That Apply to AI Security

Model Rule 1.1 — Competence
You must have a reasonable understanding of the capabilities and limitations of any AI tool you use. You don’t need to become a technologist, but you must understand enough to evaluate whether the tool is appropriate and whether adequate safeguards are in place.
Model Rule 1.6 — Confidentiality of Information
You must make “reasonable efforts to prevent the inadvertent or unauthorized disclosure of, or unauthorized access to, information relating to the representation of a client.” Before entering any client information into an AI tool, you must evaluate the risk that it could be disclosed to or accessed by others — both inside and outside your firm. This includes understanding whether the AI tool “learns” from your inputs and might surface that information to other users.
Model Rule 1.4 — Communication
If asked by a client how you conducted your work, or whether AI was used, you must answer truthfully. Some engagement agreements or outside-counsel guidelines may require affirmative disclosure. Opinion 512 suggests that boilerplate consent in engagement letters is not adequate — informed consent should be specific.
Model Rules 5.1 & 5.3 — Supervision
You must establish clear policies regarding the firm’s permissible use of AI, provide training on ethical and practical risks, and ensure that both lawyers and non-lawyer assistants conform to the rules when using AI tools.

What This Means for Your OpenClaw Setup

Opinion 512’s confidentiality requirements translate directly into security measures. Specifically, you need to be able to answer “yes” to all of the following:

  • Is the system that runs OpenClaw protected from unauthorized access?
  • Are the AI provider API keys (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.) stored securely and not exposed?
  • If running in the cloud, is access restricted to authorized personnel only?
  • Is client data transmitted over encrypted connections?
  • Do you understand whether the AI provider retains or trains on your inputs?
  • Have you reviewed the AI provider’s Terms of Service and privacy policy?
  • Do you have a documented process for what happens if the system is compromised?

The sections below explain exactly how your developer implements each of these protections.

Scenario A: Running OpenClaw on Your Office Computer

A “local” or “closed system” setup means OpenClaw runs directly on a computer in your office, not in the cloud. This is the most private option because your data never leaves your network — but it still requires proper security.

1. Encrypt Your Machine

Full-disk encryption ensures that if your computer is stolen or accessed without authorization, the data on it — including any OpenClaw conversation history and cached files — is unreadable.

A

Mac (FileVault): Go to System Settings → Privacy & Security → FileVault and turn it on. Your Mac will encrypt the entire drive. Save the recovery key in a secure location (not on the Mac itself).

B

Windows (BitLocker): Go to Settings → Privacy & Security → Device Encryption (or search for “BitLocker”). Turn it on and save your recovery key to your Microsoft account or print it and store it securely.

Why This Matters Under Model Rule 1.6, a stolen unencrypted laptop containing client-related AI conversation logs would constitute a failure to make “reasonable efforts” to prevent unauthorized access.

2. Lock Down the Network

OpenClaw uses a web-based interface that runs on a “port” on your computer — think of it like a door. By default, that door might be open to anyone on your office Wi-Fi network.

What your developer configures:

  • OpenClaw listens only on localhost (127.0.0.1), meaning only your computer can access it — not other devices on the network
  • Your operating system firewall is enabled and blocks all incoming connections to the OpenClaw port
  • If you need to access OpenClaw from another device (e.g., your phone), your developer sets up a VPN or SSH tunnel rather than opening the port to the network
# Your developer ensures OpenClaw binds to localhost only:
HOST=127.0.0.1    # Only this computer can connect
PORT=3000          # The "door" number

# NOT this (which allows anyone on the network):
HOST=0.0.0.0       # ← DANGEROUS for a law office

3. Protect Your API Keys

OpenClaw connects to AI services (like OpenAI or Anthropic) using “API keys” — essentially passwords that grant access to those services and are tied to your billing account. If someone obtains your API key, they can use the AI service at your expense and potentially access conversation data.

What your developer configures:

  • API keys are stored in a protected .env file with restricted read permissions — only your user account can read it
  • Keys are never placed directly in code, configuration files that get shared, or anywhere visible
  • If you use a version control system (like Git), the .env file is explicitly excluded
Critical Never share your API keys via email, Slack, or any unencrypted channel. If you need to provide a key to your developer, use a secure method such as a password manager’s sharing feature or an encrypted message.

4. Keep Everything Updated

  • Enable automatic operating system updates
  • Update OpenClaw when new versions are released (your developer can set up notifications)
  • Run antivirus/anti-malware software
  • Back up your system regularly — but ensure backups are also encrypted

Scenario B: Running OpenClaw on a Google Cloud Platform (GCP) VM

A “cloud” setup means OpenClaw runs on a virtual computer managed by Google, accessible over the internet. This is convenient (always-on, accessible from anywhere) but requires more rigorous security because the system is internet-facing.

Ethics Note Under Opinion 512, running client-adjacent AI tools on third-party cloud infrastructure means you are effectively outsourcing data handling. The ABA guidance on outsourcing (Formal Opinions 08-451 and 477R) applies: you must understand the vendor’s security practices, use confidentiality agreements where appropriate, and maintain reasonable oversight.

1. SSH & Access Control

“SSH” stands for Secure Shell — it’s the encrypted channel your developer uses to manage the cloud server. Think of it as a secure remote-control connection. Properly configuring SSH is the single most important step in cloud security.

What your developer configures:

1

Key-based authentication only. Instead of passwords (which can be guessed), your developer uses a cryptographic key pair. The private key stays on their machine; the public key goes on the server. No password ever crosses the internet.

2

Password login is disabled entirely. Even if an attacker discovers the server’s address, they cannot attempt password guesses — the server will simply refuse.

3

Root access is disabled. “Root” is the all-powerful administrator account. Your developer creates a separate, limited account and disables direct root login. This means even if the limited account were compromised, the attacker would face an additional barrier.

4

Automatic ban on repeated failures. Software called Fail2Ban monitors login attempts and automatically blocks IP addresses that fail multiple times — stopping brute-force attacks before they get anywhere.

# Key settings your developer applies in /etc/ssh/sshd_config:
PasswordAuthentication no       # No password guessing
PermitRootLogin no             # No all-powerful access
MaxAuthTries 3                  # Lock out after 3 failures
ClientAliveInterval 300        # Disconnect idle sessions

2. Firewall Rules

A firewall controls which “doors” (ports) on the server are open to the internet. Your developer configures this both at the GCP level (VPC firewall rules) and on the server itself.

What your developer configures:

Port Purpose Who Can Access
22 (SSH) Developer management access Only your developer’s IP address
443 (HTTPS) OpenClaw web interface Only your office IP address (or via VPN)
All others — Blocked 
# GCP firewall rule — allows HTTPS only from your office:
gcloud compute firewall-rules create allow-openclaw \
  --direction=INGRESS \
  --action=ALLOW \
  --rules=tcp:443 \
  --source-ranges="YOUR_OFFICE_IP/32" \
  --target-tags=openclaw-server

# On-server firewall (UFW) — belt and suspenders:
sudo ufw default deny incoming
sudo ufw allow from YOUR_OFFICE_IP to any port 443
sudo ufw allow from DEVELOPER_IP to any port 22
sudo ufw enable
Plain English Imagine your server is a building. The firewall locks every door except two: one for your developer (SSH) and one for you (the web interface). Both doors are further restricted so that only people coming from recognized addresses can enter.

3. Secrets Management on GCP

Google Cloud provides a dedicated service called Secret Manager for storing sensitive values like API keys. This is significantly more secure than storing keys in a file on the server.

# Your developer stores each key in GCP Secret Manager:
gcloud secrets create openai-api-key \
  --data-file=./openai-key.txt

# The application retrieves it at runtime — the key
# never sits in a file on disk:
gcloud secrets versions access latest \
  --secret=openai-api-key

Why this is better than a .env file:

  • Keys are encrypted at rest by Google’s infrastructure
  • Access is controlled by IAM (Identity and Access Management) — only the OpenClaw service account can read the keys
  • Every access is logged, creating an audit trail
  • Keys can be rotated without redeploying the application

4. Logging & Monitoring

Opinion 512 requires you to make “reasonable efforts” to prevent unauthorized access. Logging provides the evidence that those efforts are in place, and alerts you if something goes wrong.

What your developer configures:

  • GCP Cloud Audit Logs — records every login, configuration change, and secret access
  • Alerting policies — emails you and your developer if there are repeated failed login attempts, unexpected access from new IP addresses, or any access to secret keys outside of normal operations
  • Log retention — logs are retained for a minimum of 1 year (configurable per your firm’s retention policy)
Best Practice Ask your developer to set up a monthly security summary email. This gives you documented evidence of ongoing monitoring — valuable both for your own peace of mind and as proof of “reasonable efforts” under Model Rule 1.6.

Local vs. Cloud: Side-by-Side Comparison

Consideration Local (Office Computer) GCP Cloud VM
Data location On your premises Google data center (you choose region)
Internet exposure None (if configured correctly) Internet-facing; requires firewall
Access from outside office Requires VPN setup Available anywhere (with IP restriction)
Uptime Only when computer is on 24/7 with 99.95% SLA
Physical security Your responsibility Google’s data center security
Secrets management .env file with restricted permissions GCP Secret Manager (encrypted, audited)
Audit logging Basic OS logs Comprehensive Cloud Audit Logs
Outsourcing concerns (Opinion 512) Minimal — data stays local Must review Google Cloud ToS and DPA
Estimated monthly cost $0 (uses existing hardware) $15–$50/month
Best for Solo practitioners; highest sensitivity Firms needing 24/7 access; multiple users

Security Checklist

Print this page and confirm each item with your developer. Keep the completed checklist on file as documentation of your “reasonable efforts.”

General (Both Scenarios)

  • All AI provider API keys are stored securely (not hardcoded, not emailed)
  • You have reviewed the Terms of Service and Privacy Policy for each AI provider (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.)
  • You understand whether the AI provider retains or trains on your input data
  • You have a key rotation schedule (every 90 days recommended)
  • You have an incident response plan (what to do if keys are leaked or the system is breached)
  • Your firm has a written AI usage policy that addresses OpenClaw
  • Staff who use OpenClaw have been trained on what information may and may not be entered

Local System

  • Full-disk encryption is enabled (FileVault or BitLocker)
  • OpenClaw binds to localhost only
  • OS firewall is enabled and blocks incoming connections to the OpenClaw port
  • Automatic OS updates are enabled
  • Antivirus software is running and up to date
  • Computer requires a password/biometric to unlock from sleep

GCP Cloud VM

  • SSH uses key-based authentication only (passwords disabled)
  • Root login is disabled; a non-root admin account is used
  • Fail2Ban is installed and active
  • GCP firewall rules restrict SSH to developer IP only
  • GCP firewall rules restrict HTTPS to your office IP (or VPN range)
  • API keys are stored in GCP Secret Manager (not in files on the VM)
  • Cloud Audit Logs are enabled with alerting policies
  • HTTPS/TLS is configured for the web interface (no unencrypted HTTP)
  • Google Cloud Data Processing Addendum (DPA) is reviewed and on file
  • Monthly security summary reports are scheduled
